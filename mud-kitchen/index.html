A weekend backyard build

The Toddler Mud Kitchen

A cheap, sturdy, smooth-sanded outdoor mud kitchen you can build in a weekend with basic power tools, scaled low for a 2- and 4-year-old. Full materials, cut list, scaled blueprints, and step-by-step instructions.

36 × 16-16½ × 16" ages 2-6 ~$40-80 beginner a weekend

A mud kitchen makes a great first woodworking project: it's forgiving (nobody minds a wonky leg), it's cheap, and the kids will use it hard. This plan builds a four-leg table at toddler height with a flat worktop, a drop-in basin for water and mud, a lower storage shelf, and an optional backboard with hooks for hanging utensils.

The default height is 16": low enough for a 2-year-old to reach into, and a 4-year-old only has to bend a little. Wide and low like this, it's hard to tip over. Want it taller for older kids? Pick the counter height you want, then cut the legs the thickness of your top shorter than that (for this ¾" plywood top and a 24-26" counter, cut 23¼-25¼" legs). At taller heights, widen the footprint or keep the lower shelf loaded; tall narrow tables tip more easily.

1 Tools you'll need

  • Drill/driver + bits + a countersink bit (so screw heads sit flush, with no sharp points)
  • Jigsaw: the one must-have beyond a drill, for cutting the basin hole
  • A saw for straight cuts: circular saw, miter saw, or hand saw (tip: most home stores cut lumber and plywood to size for free; bring the cut list and skip most of the sawing)
  • Orbital sander or sandpaper + block (80 then 120 grit)
  • Tape measure, pencil, a square, and clamps (2-4; a huge help when working solo)

2 Materials & wood choice

Recommended wood: cedar. It resists rot and insects on its own, stays light, sands soft (so it's gentle on small hands), and has no added pressure-treatment chemicals. Cedar fence pickets are the cheapest way to buy it. There are two ways to go:

Option A: Cheapest (cedar)

Top, shelf, rails, and stretchers from cedar fence pickets; legs from 2×2. Use the Option A shopping list and cut list below.

~$35-55

Trade-off: small gaps between top boards. Naturally weather-resistant.

Option B: Sturdiest top (plywood)

Top & shelf from ¾" exterior plywood; frame from 2×2 + 1×4. The blueprints and plywood cut diagram show this version.

~$55-80

Solid flat surface, easiest basin cutout. Use the Option B cut list below.

On pressure-treated wood: modern PT lumber is generally fine for outdoor structures, but skip it for the worktop surface kids touch constantly. Cedar or sealed plywood is the smoother, safer choice for kid-handled surfaces. Using PT only for ground-contact legs is a fine compromise.

Shopping list (Option A: cedar picket version)

  • 6-7 cedar fence pickets, usually about 5/8" × 5½" × 6': top boards, slatted shelf, rails, stretchers, and cleats. Six can work if you skip the backboard and cut carefully; seven is the beginner-friendly buy and covers the optional backboard rail.
  • 1× eight-foot 2×2: legs only. Buy 2× eight-foot 2×2 if you want the backboard uprights or extra mistake margin.
  • One plastic dishpan / washing-up tub, ~13×11×4": buy this first, you'll trace it to cut the hole across the top boards.
  • Exterior wood screws ("deck screws"): a small box each of 2½" and 1⅝"; add a small pack of 1" screws for thin picket-to-picket attachments.
  • Exterior wood glue (e.g. Titebond III)
  • 4 screw-in cup hooks (backboard, optional; use short hooks and check the back for sharp points)
  • Exterior finish is optional for cedar, but useful on end grain and screw holes; add optional thrift-store bowls, pots, utensils

Measure the pickets you buy. Three 5½"-wide boards make a 16½"-deep top; center that on the frame for about ¼" overhang front and back, or trim one board if exact 16" depth matters. For the shelf, use two lengthwise picket boards instead of one plywood panel.

Shopping list (Option B)

  • 2× eight-foot 2×2: legs + optional backboard uprights
  • 2× eight-foot 1×4: rails and stretchers (buy a third as beginner spare; required if you want the backboard)
  • One 4'×4' half-sheet of ¾" exterior plywood: worktop + shelf
  • One plastic dishpan / washing-up tub, ~13×11×4": buy this first, you'll trace it to cut the hole
  • Exterior wood screws ("deck screws"): a small box each of 2½" and 1⅝"
  • Exterior wood glue (e.g. Titebond III)
  • 4 screw-in cup hooks (backboard, optional)
  • Exterior finish (see section 7), plus optional thrift-store bowls, pots, utensils

5 Build steps

Before assembly: sand every piece, including faces, edges, and especially ends, with 80 then 120 grit. Round over all corners until they feel smooth to a bare hand. With toddlers, that's the step that matters most. Always pre-drill and countersink so heads sit flush, and add a bead of exterior glue to every joint. Use 2½" screws for 2×2-to-rail frame joints and 2×2-to-2×2 backboard joints. Use 1⅝" screws for Option B plywood attachments, and 1" screws for Option A picket-to-picket attachments.

  1. Build the two end frames

    Each end = 2 legs + 2 of the 13" rails, forming a flat ladder. Lay 2 legs with their inside faces 13" apart (outside width 16"). The rails fit between the legs. Attach the top rail flush with the leg tops, and the bottom rail with its bottom edge about 2" above the ground. Drive 2 screws through each 2×2 leg into the end of each rail; check square. Make two identical frames.

  2. Connect the ends with the 33" stretchers

    Stand both frames upright with 33" between the inside faces of the end legs. The stretchers fit between the legs along the front and back: two at the top (flush with leg tops; these carry the worktop) and two at the bottom, level with the bottom rails. Drive screws through the 2×2 legs into the stretcher ends. Glue + 2 screws per end. You now have a rigid base. Check that it doesn't wobble.

  3. Drop in the shelf

    For Option B, screw one 4 × 1½" offcut cleat to the inside face of each bottom rail, with the cleat top edge flush with the lower rail's top edge; this puts the shelf surface about 6" above the ground. Place the cleats near the four corners so the shelf overlaps each cleat by about ½". Drop the 32½ × 12½" plywood shelf onto the cleats, trim/sand if it is tight, and run a few 1⅝" screws down into them. For Option A, use the full-width cedar cleat blocks and set the two 32½" shelf boards lengthwise on top, centered across the depth with small gaps for drainage; fasten with 1" screws. Drill a couple of ½" drainage holes if you like.

  4. Cut the basin hole

    Before fastening the worktop, set it on the base and mark where the frame rails sit underneath. Lift it off, set the tub upside-down where you want it (toward the back, clear of those rails), and trace the rim. For Option A, lay the three top boards in their final order and clamp them together before tracing. Draw a second line about 1" inside as a starting cut line. Drill a ½" starter hole inside it, cut with the jigsaw, then test-fit and enlarge the opening slowly until the tub body drops through and the rim catches. Sand the cut edge smooth; seal it during finishing.

  5. Attach the worktop

    For Option B, center the 36 × 16" plywood worktop on top, flush or with a tiny even overhang, and screw down with 1⅝" screws. For Option A, center the three 36" cedar top boards on the frame, then screw each board down with 1" screws where it crosses cedar rails/stretchers and 1⅝" screws where it crosses the 2×2 legs. Countersink well.

  6. Add the backboard (optional)

    Screw the two 24" uprights to the back legs with 2½" screws, about 11" overlapping the legs and about 12" rising above the worktop. Add the 36" rail across the outside face of their tops, then screw in short cup hooks for spoons, ladles, and sieves. Check the back for sharp hook points.

6 Safety checklist

  • All edges and corners rounded and sanded. Run a bare hand over everything
  • No screw points protruding; all heads countersunk flush
  • Table doesn't wobble or tip. Load the low shelf with heavy buckets to keep the weight low; place on flat ground
  • Basin cut edge sanded smooth and sealed; tub seated securely
  • Finish fully cured before the kids dig in
  • Re-check screws and sand any rough spots once a season

7 Finishing

Cedar version

If you build Option A from cedar, you can leave it unfinished. It weathers to a soft silver-gray and resists rot on its own. Still sand and round every edge.

Plywood version

For Option B, seal all plywood faces and edges with a water-based exterior sealer (low-VOC), especially the basin cutout, drainage holes, shelf edges, and screw holes. Thin coats; let it fully cure (a few days). Avoid solvent-heavy stains on surfaces kids handle.

8 Easy upgrades for later

  • A second basin hole: one for water, one for mud "prep"
  • An old faucet screwed to the backboard (play-only; looks like magic to kids)
  • A chalkboard-painted panel for "menus"
  • A small bucket hung on a hook for tools
